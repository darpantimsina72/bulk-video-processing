The goal is to generate a Bengali (Bangla) dubbing script for the English talk given by Sadhguru (Including any other speakers who question or converse with him). The SRT file text will be given at the bottom.

for ease, the srt file is formatted in the below format:
[4.139s] A little pause... and we are back. [-0.033s]

[4.139s] at the beginning of the subtitle denotes the duration of that subtitle "A little pause... and we are back."
[-0.033s] at the beginning of the subtitle denotes the gap after that subtitle, before the next subtitle starts. if gap is zero or negative, that means there is no gap, the next subtitle is starting immediately.


The SRT Temporal Blueprint: Mapping Threads to Pulses

Instead of a plain transcript, you are receiving an SRT file. This file acts as the absolute structural blueprint for the dubbing script. Think of the SRT timestamps as the rigid framework of the loom.

On-Signals (Spoken Pulses / Containers): The actual text block within a subtitle timestamp. This is continuous speech.

Off-Signals (Silence Gaps / Spaces): The time gap between the end timestamp of one subtitle and the start timestamp of the next. This is a physical pause.

CRITICAL RULE: The temporal blueprint overrides grammatical intuition. You must map the White Threads (thought units) onto these pulses and dictate the placement of your Green Threads (punctuation) strictly based on the On/Off signals, handling the mapping in the following ways:

Before assessing case 1 or 2 or 3, first you must analyze and create the groups of subtitles, so that each group denotes one meaningful chunk or one thought unit of a complete thought.


Case 1: One thought unit stretched across multiple pulses (e.g., 1 meaningful chunk or thought unit spoken over 3 subtitles):
Do not smooth this out. You must weave a Green Thread (specifically the ... indicating a pause) exactly where the temporal gap occurs between the subtitles, even if it awkwardly breaks a Bengali clause in half. The pause must reflect the speaker's hesitation or deliberate pacing.

Note that, only in this case, you can re arrange the sequence of these non-meaningful words/phrases that are building blocks of the meaningful chunk or a thought unit that was spread across multiple pulses, so that the thought unit or the meaningful chunk is formed in a natural way. Here still the pulses are separated by ... but they are resequenced to form a natural flow to form the meaningful chunk or thought unit.

This applies only inside of a meaningful chunk/thought unit. The sequence of the meaningful chunks and thought units that form a complete thought remains same.
In short, The "Holistic Mapping" Rule for case 1:
When one thought unit or meaningful chunk is stretched across multiple pulses (pulses separated by ...), do not translate pulse-by-pulse. Instead:

Synthesize: First, translate the entire English thought unit into a natural, complete Bengali sentence or clause.

Distribute: Take that complete Bengali translation and "pour" it into the available pulse containers (the SRT timestamps).

Fragment Naturally: When distributing the Bengali text across pulses, ensure that the text within each pulse forms a natural phonetic chunk (a phrase or sub-part) in Bengali.

Transposition: Since Bengali is a Subject-Object-Verb (SOV) language and English is SVO, you must rearrange the words across the ... markers so the verb lands in the appropriate pulse for the Bengali flow, while ensuring the overall meaning matches the duration of the total pulses used.

The "Bridge" Principle: Each segment between the ... should feel like a natural "breath" or a logical sub-component of the thought, not a random collection of words.

Example of how to handle the "broken" input:

English Pulses: the worship of the ... divine ... in the feminine form ... was hugely prevalent

Wrong (Literal): এর উপাসনা ... ঈশ্বরকে ... নারী রূপে ... ব্যাপকভাবে প্রচলিত ছিল (This feels broken).

Correct (Mapped): ঈশ্বরকে ... নারী রূপে ... উপাসনা করা ... ব্যাপকভাবে প্রচলিত ছিল (Each pulse now contains a logical Bengali phrase that builds toward the meaningful chunk/thought unit/conclusion).


Case 2: Multiple thought units compressed into one pulse (e.g., 2-3 short sentences spoken rapidly in 1 subtitle):
If the SRT shows continuous speech with no off-signal gaps, do not inject ... pauses just because the grammar suggests a new sentence. Translate the White Threads to flow rapidly together, using commas (,) in-between thought units and ; between different thoughts to maintain continuous structural phrasing to match the unbroken momentum of the speaker.

Case 3: Overlapping boundaries (e.g., a pulse contains the end of thought A and the start of thought B):
Translate the transition naturally with appropriate boundary punctuation, but reserve your heavy pause punctuation (... or -) only for where the actual SRT gap (Off-Signal) occurs.

How Sadhguru Delivers His Speech (Structurally)

 A. Thought-by-thought delivery (NOT sentence-by-sentence)

Sadhguru does not speak in grammatically complete sentences.

He speaks in:

thought units

idea chunks

pause-driven fragments

Example (English original style):

“Life is not a problem to be solved…

it is a reality to be experienced.”

This is two thoughts, not one sentence.

 Translation rule:

 Translate by thought, not by grammar.



 B. Pause is part of meaning

Sadhguru uses silence as punctuation.

He often pauses:

before the punchline

after a deep question

before contradiction (“No…”)

 Translation rule:

 Preserve pause points even if grammar feels “broken”.



 C. Question → Silence → Answer pattern

He frequently does:

Ask a simple question

Pause

Turn the question upside down

Example:

“Do you want peace?

Or do you want to control everything?”

 Translation rule:

 Keep questions short and spoken, not literary.

Below is the information regarding the green threads:

now as we weave the white threads to form the sentences, we want to weave in the green threads (punctuation marks) as per the below rules.



… indicates a pause in between the parts that makes a meaningful chunk or a clause.

, indicates a pause between meaningful chunks that make up the sentence.

; indicates connected sentences.

Hyphen (-) indicates additional information related to the main idea of the sentence.




we connect building parts with … to make up the internal part.

we connect the internal parts with - to make up the subpart.

we connect the subparts with ; to make up the body part.



for example, the punctuation marks should be weaved like this:

building part …  building part (one internal part)- another internal part - one more internal part; internal part - building part …  building part (another internal part);

internal part -  building part …  building part (one internal part)

subpart; subpart; internal part - internal part; building part…building part…building part; subpart

Below is the information regarding the Pink threads:

Below is the information regarding the orange threads:



Each orange thread is the paragraph break that happens in the dubbing script.

Below are the rules where to weave in the orange thread.





When the subject changes (e.g., from speaking about 'Nature' to 'Humans')

When the focus moves from one concept to a completely new idea.

Switch from Description to Reason

Switch from one Context to another.

Switch from Positive to Negative contrast

Split the Question from its Answer.

Before a quoted thought or dialogue starts.

When a sentence structure repeats with a different subject.

When the speaker or the person being quoted changes.

When it changes from a core subject to its explanation using an example, illustration, or analogy.





And the sentences that occur within one para can be made into one continuous paragraph. no need to keep them as separate lines within the paragraph.

Example:

in the above it is one paragraph, but it is three lines in a paragraph. the linebreaks within the paragraph can be removed and it can be made into continuous sentences for ease of reading as shown below:

CRITICAL FORMATTING RULE: Continuous Text Blocks



No Soft Breaks: Do not use line breaks or newlines within a paragraph for visual formatting.



Single Line Constraint: Each paragraph block must be rendered as one single, continuous line of text.



Separation: Use a double line break only between different paragraphs.

Scenario 4:

You have three additional tools to shorten without losing the actual intent that is being conveyed:

Sandhi (সন্ধি / Vowel Merging): Merge vowels and adjacent sounds where Bengali naturally permits it (e.g., "যে আছে" → "যেই আছে" / "যাঁ-আছে"-style elisions; "করিয়া" → "করে"). You save a syllable and a glottal stop (pause), making the flow smoother for the voice actor.

Samasa (সমাস / Compound Nouns): Instead of "প্রাণের সংকেত" (The signs of life), you use "প্রাণ-সংকেত". It sounds tighter and cuts the length significantly.

Half sentence: sometimes while speaking we just say the main info part given the context is already established by previous sentences. if needed use this approach.

Verb Fusing / Contracted Forms: Use contracted spoken forms — "করিতেছি" → "করছি"; "যাইতেছি" → "যাচ্ছি"; "হয় না" → "হয়না". It turns a concept into a single action and matches natural Bengali colloquial speech.
