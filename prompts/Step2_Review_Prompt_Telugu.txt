### **PART 3: SMART ELLIPSIS & SYLLABLE ECONOMY (వ్యావహారిక భాష / Conversational Telugu)**

To achieve the "Short Notation" of spoken Telugu, apply the following Linguistic Heuristics:

1. **Subject Deletion (Zero Anaphora):** If the subject (I, You, He, She) is the same as the previous sentence, **delete the pronoun** in the current sentence wherever Telugu permits — Telugu verb endings often carry person/honorific information. 2. **Topic-Comment Structure:** If a noun (the "Topic") was the main focus of the previous sentence, do not repeat it. Jump straight to the "Comment" (the new information).
3. **When NOT to Delete:** RETAIN words if: the subject switches, the word provides emphasis, or it introduces new information.
4. **Syllable Economy:** Use spoken (వ్యావహారిక) forms over literary (గ్రాంధిక): "చేస్తున్నాను" → "చేస్తన్నాను"; "వెళ్తున్నారు" → "వెళ్తారు"; "అయిపోయింది" → "అయ్యింది"; "చేసి" → "చేసి". Use 'అందుకే' instead of formal 'అందువల్ల'. Use 'కాబట్టి' / 'ఎందుకంటే' instead of formal 'కావున' where natural.
5. **Ignore English Crutch Words:** "Do not translate English conversational fillers (like 'So', 'Well', 'You know', 'And then') if they do not add logical value to the sentence. Drop them entirely to save syllables."

### **PART 5: INTENTION IDENTIFICATION**

Based on the context in which this sentence is occurring, and based on the picture that is being created before and after this sentence, identify the intention of the speaker—what he actually is trying to convey. Rather than just relying on the words and doing a word-to-word translation, grasp the intention based on the context and express it directly in Telugu in a colloquial way. No need to follow the exact word-to-word translation. The intention needs to be expressed here.

Strict Sequence Preservation (No Clause Flipping): You must absolutely preserve the chronological sequence of the English thought units and clauses. Do not reorder cause-and-effect structures or flip the halves of a sentence just to make the Telugu sound more grammatically traditional. The "Master Telugu Orator" instruction applies only at the phrase and vocabulary level. You must express the deep intention of the phrase without altering the order in which the speaker chose to deliver those thoughts. Even if a sequence (like stating the Effect before the Cause) sounds slightly unnatural in formal Telugu, keep the original sequence intact to match the audio timing.

Note: this is not about semantic validation. to accommodate in the timeslot available for dubbing - many sentences are optimized into a non grammatical syntax. the goal is not to unpack them, doing so will actually make the sentence longer thereby creating unintended overlaps in the dubbed audio. the goal is to identify places where the speaker's intent is totally or significantly different than what is being expressed by the current translation. in such cases correct the point that is being conveyed - At the same time make sure you don't make it longer than the existing length.

### **PART 6: SYNTAX OF REPORTED SPEECH (BRINGING THE VERB TO THE END)**

When quoting someone's words or expressing an internal thought process, bring the verb part to the end of the sentence. Remember that the ellipses (…), hyphens (-), and semicolons (;) within the quoted portion must still be preserved.

Crucial Exception (Direct Quotes vs. Observations): Apply this rule only when the speaker is explicitly quoting someone's exact words (e.g., "He said, '...'") or explicitly stating a specific internal monologue (e.g., "I thought to myself, '...'"). Do not apply this rule to general observations about society or human behavior (e.g., "People think that..."). If the speaker is making an observation rather than reporting a direct quote, do not bring the verb to the end. Maintain the original sequence of the thought units.

**Example 1:**

* Original: వాళ్ళు <T3>అనుకుంటున్నారు<T3> ఇప్పుడు ఆధ్యాత్మికత అంటే హిమాలయాలకు వెళ్ళాలి లేదా కాశీకి వెళ్ళాలి... లేదా అన్నీ వదిలేయాలి

You have three main tools to shorten without losing the actual intent that is being conveyed:

Sandhi (సంధి / Vowel Merging): Merge vowels and adjacent sounds where Telugu naturally permits it (e.g., "అది ఉంది" → "అదుంది"; "చేస్తున్నాను" → "చేస్తన్నాను"). You save a syllable and a glottal stop (pause), making the flow smoother for the voice actor.
Samasa (సమాసం / Compound Nouns): Instead of "జీవితపు సంకేతాలు" (The signs of life), you use "జీవిత-సంకేతాలు". It sounds tighter and cuts the length significantly.
Verb Fusing / Contracted Forms: Use contracted spoken forms — "చేస్తున్నాను" → "చేస్తన్నాను"; "వెళ్తున్నాను" → "వెళ్తన్నాను"; "అయిపోయింది" → "అయ్యింది". It turns a concept into a single action.
